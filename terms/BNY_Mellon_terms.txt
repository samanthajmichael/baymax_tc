   <h3><span class="font-publico-pro">Online Security</span></h3>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The Internet is one of the most powerful communication tools available, making it possible to share information instantly, any time of the day or night, around the globe.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Criminals have capitalized on the broad power and wide availability of the Internet and electronic mail (e-mail) to defraud unsuspecting people. It is critical that each of us maintain constant vigilance over the way we use the Internet and all forms of electronic communication.<br /></span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The Bank of New York  maintains active oversight of all of our systems as part of our efforts to protect the security and privacy of client information.<br /></span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">If you have any concerns or questions, please contact your Bank of New York  representative.<br /></span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The Bank of New York  does not contact its clients or anyone else by e-mail to confirm credit card or financial transactions, or to confirm or request personal account information or any other type of sensitive information.</span></p>
<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Protect Yourself<br /></span></h4>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">To help protect yourself and your personal data, do not trust any e-mail communications that request your personal information.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Criminals can be convincing. They make their fraudulent e-mails look like they come from legitimate sources. They publish fake websites that use designs, information and programming stolen from their rightful owners. Cyber criminals use methods to impersonate you over the phone to arrange funds transfers, or imitate communications from the financial institution to verify transactions, or initiate other changes to your account. Don't fall for their ploys.<br /></span></p>
<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro">E-mail and Website Scams<br /></span></h4>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">E-mail is by far the most popular way for criminals to try to get your attention — and your personal information. An e-mail may direct you to a website designed and operated by criminals to trick you into revealing such information. Therefore, treat e-mail from someone you don't know the same way you would treat a telemarketing call from someone you don't know: don't necessarily believe what you're being told.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Fraudulent e-mails and websites are created every day to attempt to steal personal information. It's called &quot;phishing&quot; — a variation of the word &quot;fishing.&quot; There are limitless variations of these online scams, so the best defense is education and a healthy dose of skepticism. A few misleading and deceptive techniques in use include the following:<br /></span></p>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The e-mail or website may appear to be genuine.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">It may include a logo that appears legitimate.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">It may ask you to click on a link to go to a website — the website address may, at first glance, appear legitimate and imply importance.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The e-mail or website may ask for you to supply account numbers, Social Security numbers, personal identification numbers (PINs), passwords or credit card numbers.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The e-mail or website may even already contain some of this information and is asking you to confirm the data.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">You are right to be suspicious of any e-mail or website asking you to supply or confirm any personal information.</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">As technology and one's ability to detect these scams improves, so, unfortunately, do the criminals. The latest attacks do not even require you to do anything. Merely opening the e-mail can launch &quot;hidden&quot; software — a virus, &quot;spyware&quot; or other malicious code — that will download to and reside on your computer. Should they go undetected, any of these programs could compromise your computer in a variety of ways, including stealing private information, redirecting your Web surfing to unscrupulous sites and transmitting information that you type on your computer directly to the criminals. Therefore you should delete all unwanted and potentially fraudulent e-mails without opening them.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Some fraudulent e-mails, spear-phishing attempts for example, can be very well done and very convincing. These are often created by more sophisticated and more determined criminals who are highly motivated to succeed. More generally, however, most fraudulent e-mails and websites established for fraud may frequently be characterized by the following:</span></p>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Misspellings and other typographical errors</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Poor grammar</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Urgent messages in the e-mail subject line</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Random characters in the e-mail subject line or body</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">&quot;Fuzzy&quot; logos, or logos that are distorted</span></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Tricks of the Trade<br /></span></h4>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">There are a number of common e-mail scams of which you should be aware. While this list is by no means exhaustive, recent e-mail tricks include:</span></p>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro"><b>Creating a sense of panic.</b> E-mails threatening loss of account access, loss of credit, foreclosure, etc., are trying to get you to panic enough to lose your common sense and fall for their scam. Don't panic; when in doubt, call your financial institution.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro"><b>Referencing a recent transaction.</b> Vaguely worded e-mails referring to a &quot;recent transaction&quot; that you need to go online and verify, or for which you need to provide additional account information, are also just trying to get you to fall for their scam.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro"><b>Confirming your account information.</b> It's possible that the criminals think they already have your account numbers, password, etc., and all they need is your confirmation. They could have guessed, they could have bought it, they could have stolen it. Don't give them the help they need to use it. Do not respond to confirmation-of-information requests received via an e-mail that you are not expecting or that could possibly be fraudulent. This includes both not responding via e-mail and not visiting a website to confirm the information.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro"><b>You're a winner!</b> &quot;Just send money to cover the costs/fees/taxes and you can claim your prize.&quot; If you do respond to this solicitation, the thief will have your money as well as your credit or debit card information if you pay online, or your checking account number and bank routing information if you pay by check. A legitimate contest will never make you pay to receive the prize.</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro"><b>Your donation is needed.</b> Many criminals act like they are actually charities and request your donations, appealing to your emotions and taking advantage of tragedies and natural disasters. Be careful when making charitable donations. Many legitimate organizations now accept donations online. But exercise caution and only donate via a non-profit organization's website directly, or if you are absolutely sure of the company through which you are donating.</span></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Website Spoofing<br /></span></h4>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">&quot;Spoofing&quot; is another trick used by criminals. Criminals steal a website's code — the technical programming that makes the website work — and use it to create a fake website that &quot;spoofs&quot; or appears to be the legitimate site.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The difficulty for unsuspecting consumers is that these sites look legitimate. To help protect yourself, be aware of how you're accessing the site.</span></p>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Don't follow a link in an unsolicited e-mail if you have any doubts about the sender (see &quot;phishing&quot;, above).</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Type all website addresses carefully, or use Favorites or Bookmarks to store frequently accessed sites — especially financial-related sites. Misspelling, even by one letter, the address of the website you are trying to access may send you to an incorrect, possibly fraudulent, website.</span></li>
</ul>
<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Lottery / Sweepstakes Letter Scams<br /></span></h4>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">If you receive a letter, accompanied by a check with a The Bank of New York Mellon brand, that claims you have won a lottery, a sweepstakes, have been chosen to be a paid &quot;secret shopper&quot; or a similar variation of a popular contest, be advised that these are scam letters and fraudulent checks. If you contact the sender as requested, you will be instructed to negotiate the check and forward the sender money through a wire transfer or money order. Please do not negotiate these checks, as they are not authentic The Bank of New York Mellon checks. If you receive one of these letters and/or checks, you should report it to your local U.S. Postal Inspection Service.</span></p>
<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Corporate Account Takeover<br /></span></h4>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Corporate account takeovers, once associated mostly with large corporations, have started to target municipalities, smaller businesses, and non-profit organizations. Thousands of businesses, small and large, have reportedly fallen victim to this type of fraud. To obtain access to business financial accounts, cyber criminals often target employees and cause the targeted individual to download and spread malicious software (or &quot;malware&quot;) which in turn steals their log-in credentials. Cyber criminals typically will accomplish this by getting you to perform some action such as open an e-mail attachment, accept a fake friend request on a social networking site, visit an already compromised legitimate website, or plug an infected USB drive into your system. All of these can install malware on your computer.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Some recent methods used to trick employees into opening the attachment or clicking on the link, have included making the e-mail appear come from a legitimate business, for example:</span></p>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">UPS (e.g., &quot;There has been a problem with your shipment.&quot;)</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Financial institutions (e.g., &quot;There is a problem with your banking account.&quot;)</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Better Business Bureaus (e.g., &quot;A complaint has been filed against you.&quot;)</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Court systems (e.g., &quot;You have been served a subpoena.&quot;)</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Cyber criminals may try to take advantage of some current event, such as a natural disasters or major sporting events. They may use credentials stolen from company websites or co-workers or executives and design the e-mail to look like it comes from a trusted source.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Once they are able to get you to download their malware, they can easily steal your account login credentials and then be able to electronically steal money from your business accounts through unauthorized wire transfers and ACH payments.</span></p>

<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Businesses and Corporate Clients Should<br /></span></h4>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Proactively use account features that may protect their accounts, such as check cashing limitations and automated payment filters</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Consider using positive pay features to limit check fraud</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Consider using debit blocks on disbursement only accounts</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Reconcile banking transactions on a daily basis</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Initiate ACH and wire transfer payments under dual control, with a transaction originator and a separate transaction authorizer</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Business and personal customers also should employ best practices to secure computer systems in their homes and business including, but not limited to:</span></p>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Utilize strong passwords with at least 10 characters that include a combination of mixed case letters, numbers and special characters</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Do not share with others usernames and passwords for online banking systems</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Use a different password for online banking sites than you might use for e-mail and other Internet activities</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Verify use of a secure session (https not http) in the browser for all online banking</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Do not &quot;script&quot; usernames and passwords for online banking to allow for automatic logins</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Install commercial anti-virus, desktop firewall, and intrusion detection software on all computer systems and apply updates regularly</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Ensure computers are patched regularly, particularly operating system and key applications with security patches</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Always activate the screensaver locking feature when you need to leave your computer unattended</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Limit administrative rights on users' workstations to help prevent the inadvertent downloading of malware or other viruses — create limited user accounts for daily use</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Where practical, carry out online banking activities from a stand-alone and locked down computer from which e-mail and Web browsing are not possible</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Never access bank, brokerage or other financial services information from public kiosks such as those found at Internet cafes, public libraries, and airports</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Educate your staff on this type of fraud scheme</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Configure routers and firewalls to deny unauthorized access to your computer or network</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Change the default passwords on all network devices regularly</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Block pop-ups</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Keep abreast of the continuous cyber threats that occur</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Engage IT support staff if staff reports any unexplained changes in the performance or behavior of computers, they experience unexpected system shutdown and restarts, or see new and unexpected toolbars or menu items</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Make sure your employees know how and to whom to report suspicious activity to within your company and at your financial institution</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Ensure your contingency plan addresses the need to recover systems suspected of compromise by malware, not just data corruption and catastrophic system/hardware failure</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Consider whether other company or personal data may have been compromised</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Contact your financial institution for more information</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Immediately notify us of any suspicious transactions, particularly ACH or wire transfers.</span></p>
<h4><span class="font-akkurat-pro"> E-mail Security</span></h4>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">BNY Mellon actively works to protect the privacy and data integrity of sensitive information while it is in our possession and control. In the course of providing services, we may exchange information with clients or their authorized representatives which is sensitive and confidential. In order to protect this information, BNY requires Highly Confidential Information (HCI), and in some cases other types of sensitive information, to be encrypted when transmitted over an open unsecured network.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">BNY provides two methods of encryption for electronic messages containing HCI sent to external recipients.</span></p>
<ul>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">Transport Layer Security (TLS)</span></li>
 <li><span class="font-akkurat-pro">BNY Secure Messaging Portal</span></li>
</ul>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro"><b>Transport Layer Security</b> is the primary and preferred method of encryption for BNY. This method of encryption allows e-mail to be automatically secured with no additional steps required by the sender and recipient. TLS requires our external partner organizations to have both an TLS capable infrastructure and a valid digital certificate for encryption. Once established, this method is the most convenient for all users and provides seamless encryption for e-mail and attachments. TLS encrypts e-mail message between servers and is designed to protect confidentiality and data integrity and is a widely recognized standard issued by the Internet Engineering Task Force (IETF) for securing transmitted data.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">If you have questions about TLS encryption or to establish an TLS relationship with your client, please contact the <a href="mailto:tlsadmin@bnymellon.com">TLS Administration Team</a>.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">If you would like to learn more about how BNY protects the privacy and data integrity of sensitive information using TLS encryption of electronic mail, please review our <a href="https://www.bnymellon.com/content/dam/bnymellon/documents/pdf/suppliers/bny-mellon-transport-layer-security-for-external-partners.pdf">Transport Layer Security for External Partners</a> guide.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro"><b>BNY Secure Messaging Portal</b> is the alternative secure encryption tool which is utilized when an external partner does not have TLS capability. The portal encapsulates a message and its attachments into an encrypted message. Once protected, the encrypted message is sent to recipients as an attachment to a plain text e-mail. The recipient uses a self-created, pre-registered password to access the notification and any attachments. This guide provides step-by-step instructions on how to register and use BNY's Secure Messaging tool.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">The BNY Secure E-mail User Guide can be obtained by contacting <a href="mailto:securemailsupport@bnymellon.com">securemailsupport@bnymellon.com</a>.</span></p>
<p><span class="font-akkurat-pro">If you have questions about BNY's Secure Messaging Portal, please contact the Secure Messaging Team at <a href="mailto:securemailsupport@bnymellon.com">securemailsupport@bnymellon.com</a>.</span></p>
